Winter 2023 Work Term Report

  Dear reader, the following article will detail my time at Clear Software where I spent four months as a web developer during the winter of 2023. I thoroughly enjoyed my time and hope that this article gives you some insight into what my day to day experiences consisted of and what exactly made Clear Software such an enjoyable place to work at.

<About-Clear-Software/>

  Clear software is a relatively small company with two core developers who have been leading the company since 2009. These two have been working together for a long time and are brilliant developers, both attended Waterloo for their undergrads in comp-sci and math related fields. Clear also consists of a QA department and depending on how many interns are present, the team ranges from 5-8 people in total. Clear Software is a custom software shop, creating solutions for whatever platform or industry is required. The team has worked on significant projects ranging from low-level hardware and robots to fully fledged and scalable web apps. As for where I would be working, that would be in my office at home as Clear is currently operating fully remotely. Once upon a time they did have an office in the Waterloo region, but when Covid shut everything down that was no longer necessary.

<Goals/>

  Now that you’re in the loop regarding what Clear Software does, let’s talk about what I wanted to get out of this term. Before the term began I was tasked with producing some personal goals that would help steer me during the next four months. I knew from prior terms that these goals had to be well-suited to my time frame and they needed to be ambitious enough that I could strive for them, but also not be too far away to the point that they were unachievable. The following is what I came up with.

  • Become better at managing my time during working hours. I often struggled to find the balance between working too much, or possibly not working enough throughout the day. Other pitfalls of poor time management included working too long without taking a break and eventually fatiguing, or staying up late/waking up early to chase down a bug that I was hooked on solving. My plan was to get serious about my time and consistently take short breaks after ~30 minutes of very focused work. You might think that seems ridiculous, who needs a break after only 30 minutes anyways. But, it’s something that’s worked for me and many other individuals, particularly while studying during exam season.
  • Take on some significant chunk of a project or lead development on a large piece of functionality in a very independent way. This goal has a lot to do with me wanting to “own” some part of the codebase. It’s fun to be a bit of an expert on some area of the codebase and I think the best way to learn the ins and outs is to be there when it gets created, or better yet, be the creator!
  • Get better at understanding other people’s code and the way they write, the patterns they use, then take that information and use it to improve my own code. Similar to reading plain English, I think that it’s super valuable and important to be able to quickly and easily read other peoples’ code. Some of the most skilled devs I know can pick up a random piece of scrappy looking code and tell you what it does in a matter of seconds, it’s like a super power. With Clear being such a small company, I think it will be important to write code that will blend in well. The idea here is to write code and not have anyone be able to tell who wrote it (without using git blame, cheater!). A seamless looking code base would be easier for new people to read and makes maintenance easier too.

  That summarizes my larger goals for the term, of course I also came up with smaller ones week to week and day to day that helped me keep a solid learning trajectory throughout the term. Now that we’ve discussed my goals for the term, let’s talk about what I actually did.

<My-Job/>

  When I joined the team, Clear was in the later part of their main development phase on their latest project, prior to anything going live. The project is a large internal web based tool for one of Canada’s largest structured settlement firms. This tool will end up handling thousands of gigs worth of personal data and boasts a suite of very industry specific tools/workflows and internal management systems to help speed up the company’s various processes. I will also mention here that I worked almost exclusively in the front end, with the occasional need to work on some other areas of the code base.

  The stack consisted of MariaDB, Express, React and Node. The fun part about all of this was that 99% of our communications were done with GraphQL rather than REST APIs. I hadn’t worked with GraphQL before, and all I knew was that the requests looked really weird in comparison to RESTful stuff. Nonetheless I was excited, the first week was fairly typical onboarding stuff. I got to sink my teeth into the code base and was incredibly impressed. The lead frontend developer had done a really really good job of writing what appeared to be highly organized, clean, and maintainable code. This was very exciting as I hadn’t worked in a codebase this neat before.

  After the first week I got busy with some tickets, I was really enjoying my time. My initial PRs often had some change requests because the code base was so large, and I would end up writing new functions that were already written somewhere else and I had just missed them. After a while I got the hang of things and my PRs went smoother, I felt like I was writing pretty good code and I was absolutely putting a lot of effort in to try and make it look similar to the existing code. This paid off I think, one of the comments during my exit interview was related to this and It was very positive feedback.

  After the first month and a half or so I got my first big set of new feature tickets. This feature would consist of a lot of UI additions and it would in essence be an internal event manager that integrated with our structure of companies and employees to help make managing invites easier. I had a lot of fun with this and it went well without too many hang ups. I felt like I had done something cool and independent, and this was some part of the code that I could absolutely own per my goal up above.

  I continued working on bug fixes and tickets for some time until a new request came up. The company this project is for has a current system that is outdated and lacking a lot of features, which is why they’ve commissioned Clear to write a new one. However, one of the functionalities of this system that did work well, and would need to stick around was an automated reporting system, for various accounting and analytics related reports. I got to researching new solutions right away as the old solution was written in Microsoft Visual FoxPro and would not be sticking around. After a couple of attempts and experimenting with various solutions we wound up agreeing to use JaperSoft’s reporting tools. They have a cool report building tool and you can easily spin up a reporting server and configure it to fire off automated reports. I got part way through the implementation of this before my time was up, I hope that it all comes together nicely!

  Overall, I really enjoyed the work at Clear. The automated reporting researching/experimenting was probably my least favorite part as it was much more reading, and much less coding. I loved working on the events feature, as I could bury myself in code for multiple days at a time. I guess this has helped me discover some of the specific things that I do and do not enjoy.

<Reflections/>

  Remember those goals I mentioned above? It’s okay if you don’t, they are my goals after all. Anyhow, let’s do a little reflecting on how I did with those goals.

  My first goal was to get better at managing my time. A big part of this I found was just being surrounded by distractions while working from home. I removed a lot of the distractions, and set timers to take breaks and it worked great, I spent less time futzing around and was more productive. My second goal was to take ownership of a significant feature, I was able to achieve this with both the events feature implementation and the automated reporting research and development. I would say this was another successful goal. My final goal was to get better at reading others' code, and take what I learned and implement it into my own code. I would also say this one was a success, I received multiple pieces of feedback throughout the term in PRs and meetings saying that it was very clear that I was making strong efforts to make code that looked similar to everything that was already written. I think I was most satisfied with this goal, since It was something that took some time and effort to get right, but once I got it, the team was able to appreciate my efforts. All in, I consider this to be a very successful term.

<Summary/>

  In conclusion, I had a great time at Clear Software. I enjoyed my work as a frontend web developer, the tech stack was interesting and the work was rewarding. I created what I felt were reasonable goals and achieved all of them, although perhaps I should have been more ambitious with them. Something about shooting for the stars and landing in the clouds, being more ambitious would have raised the risk of failure but that’s probably necessary to grow more. I’ll keep that in mind for my next term as a software developer at Desire2Learn.
